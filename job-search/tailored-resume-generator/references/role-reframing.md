# Role Reframing Patterns for Career Transitions

When tailoring a resume for a role that's adjacent to (but not identical to) the candidate's current function, reframing is the core technique. The goal is to describe the *same experience* using the *target role's vocabulary* without fabricating anything.

## Support → Solutions Engineering / Pre-Sales

This is a common transition. Support engineers have deep technical skills and customer-facing experience — they just frame it differently than SEs.

| Support Framing | Solutions Engineering Framing | Why It Works |
|---|---|---|
| "Escalation resource for complex issues" | "Own the technical customer lifecycle from onboarding through integration troubleshooting" | SEs own the technical relationship end-to-end — support escalations are part of that |
| "Troubleshoot API integration issues" | "Design integrations and consultatively influence technical decisions" | Same work, different emphasis — troubleshooting reveals what "should" exist |
| "Collaborate with Engineering and Product" | "Collaborate cross-functionally with Engineering, Product, and GTM teams" | Add "GTM" — SEs sit at the intersection of technical and business |
| "Improve support workflows and documentation" | "Develop and improve playbooks, processes, and tooling" | "Playbooks" is SE/pre-sales vocabulary |
| "Mentor and train junior engineers" | "Build team capability and scalable technical engagement" | Frames mentoring as a force multiplier, not just coaching |
| "Work with Sales on customer escalations" | "Partner closely with Sales and Solutions Engineering to build tailored customer solutions" | Directly maps to SE workflow |
| "Resolve 10-12 cases daily" | "Manage multiple concurrent customer engagements, consistently meeting SLA targets" | "Engagements" sounds more SE than "tickets" |
| "Debug complex technical issues" | "Run Proofs of Concept with clear success criteria to achieve technical wins" | If they've built lab environments or reproduced issues, this maps to POC work |

## Support → Customer Success Engineer (CSE)

This is a hybrid role combining technical support depth with customer-facing enablement and advisory. CSEs operate in a **pooled support model** (not account-dedicated) and focus on driving product adoption through technical guidance, workshops, and reusable content. Key differentiator from pure support: proactive enablement, content creation, and cross-functional partnership with CSMs and AEs.

| Support Framing | Customer Success Engineer Framing | Why It Works |
|---|---|---|
| "Primary escalation resource for complex issues" | "Acted as trusted technical advisor in pooled support model, managing 15+ concurrent enterprise engagements" | "Pooled model" and "concurrent engagements" match CSE JD language exactly |
| "Troubleshoot and resolve customer issues" | "Delivered technical enablement through live Zoom workshops, office hours, and on-demand sessions" | Shows shift from reactive to proactive |
| "Collaborate with Engineering on bugs" | "Partner with Customer Success Managers and Account Executives to align technical guidance with customer business outcomes" | Adds the GTM partnership dimension |
| "Created internal documentation and playbooks" | "Built and maintained reusable enablement content: API integration guides, troubleshooting playbooks, video tutorials" | "Reusable enablement content" is CSE vocabulary |
| "Mentored junior engineers" | "Developed specialty competency in platform-aligned technologies through hands-on labs and certification, maintaining working examples for demos" | Shows continuous learning and demo capability |
| "Used data to identify trends" | "Leveraged data analytics to identify adoption gaps, prioritize enablement content, and surface insights to Product and Engineering" | Data-driven content strategy |
| "Contributed to knowledge base" | "Contributed to product documentation and learning resources, ensuring accuracy across evolving product surfaces" | Shows broader content ownership |
| "Communicated with developers and executives" | "Explained complex technical workflows (CI/CD, DevSecOps, Agile) to both technical and non-technical audiences" | Domain-specific communication |

**Critical resume moves for this pivot:**
- Summary must lead with "Customer Success Engineer" or "Technical Customer Success" — not "Support Engineer"
- Skills section: **DevSecOps/Platform expertise FIRST**, Documentation & Enablement SECOND, Support Platforms THIRD
- Experience bullets: emphasize *enablement content creation*, *workshop delivery*, *cross-functional GTM partnership*, *adoption metrics*, *specialty competency development*
- De-emphasize ticket volume, case resolution speed — these signal "support IC" not "CSE"
- Include metrics: "reduced repeat escalations by 30%", "accelerated time-to-value", "drove adoption of X feature"
- Show async collaboration experience — CSE teams are often globally distributed

**GitLab-specific CSE context:**
- GitLab CSEs support the **On-Demand Success Tier** — customers who don't have dedicated CSMs
- Pooled model means juggling multiple customers simultaneously — highlight time management and prioritization
- DevSecOps domain: source code management (SCM), CI/CD, security scanning, compliance automation
- Values: "AI as core productivity multiplier" — emphasize AI-augmented workflows
- Culture: async-first, globally distributed, handbook-driven

## Support → Technical Content Writer / Developer Docs

This pivot reframes a decade of internal documentation, playbooks, and knowledge-base articles as *content ownership and strategy*. The key move: you're not "someone who wrote docs on the side" — you're a *technical content professional who operated inside a support org*.

| Support Framing | Technical Content Writer Framing | Why It Works |
|---|---|---|
| "Wrote playbooks and troubleshooting guides" | "Owned creation and maintenance of internal documentation standards across a 50+ person organization" | Frames isolated docs as content *ownership* with organizational scale |
| "Created solution articles for knowledge base" | "Produced API integration guides, conceptual articles, and self-service resources" | Maps KRAs to writer deliverables |
| "Documented bugs with reproduction steps" | "Translated customer-reported issues into clear, actionable technical documentation for engineering teams" | Bridges the writer's "interview engineers to grasp complex topics" skill |
| "Improved support workflows and documentation" | "Established documentation standards, review processes, and quality metrics (deflection rate, time-to-resolution impact)" | Content ops framing |
| "Mentored and trained engineers" | "Collaborated cross-functionally with Product and Engineering to ship documentation in lockstep with product releases" | Shows content partnership, not just writing |
| "Contributed to Confluence/Jira knowledge base" | "Managed Git-based Docs-as-Code workflows and content management" | Puts tooling in writer vocabulary |
| "Used Mode/Grafana to analyze ticket data" | "Used data analysis to identify documentation gaps and prioritize content creation based on customer impact" | Same skill, different framing |
| "Championed AI and automation tools" | "Leveraged AI tools (LLMs, coding assistants) to accelerate content creation and automate documentation updates" | Maps directly to AI-augmented writing workflows |
| "Communicated with CPOs and developers on the same call" | "Explained complex distributed systems clearly to both technical and non-technical audiences" | Communication flexibility is a writer core skill |

**Critical resume moves for this pivot:**
- Summary must lead with "technical content professional" or equivalent — not "support engineer who also writes"
- Skills section: put Documentation & Content FIRST, Technical Domain knowledge SECOND, Support Platforms THIRD
- Experience bullets: emphasize *content ownership*, *documentation scaling*, *content quality metrics*, *cross-functional content partnership*
- Strip out or de-emphasize ticket volume, case resolution speed, on-call metrics — these signal "support IC" not "writer"

**Handling user-supplied skills requests (e.g., "include that I have Git/GitHub/Hugo/Markdown experience"):**
When the user explicitly asks to include specific skills that aren't formally documented in prior job titles, those skills MUST appear prominently in the tailored resume. This is common for career pivots where the user has side-project or self-taught experience they want represented. Technique:
1. Add them to the Technical Skills section under a Docs-as-Code or Documentation & Authoring category, listed as daily tools
2. Weave them into experience bullets where contextually plausible (e.g., "version-controlled documentation with Git-based PR workflows" for someone who uses Confluence/GitHub)
3. Add a **Portfolio & Docs-as-Code** section (between Experience and Certifications) listing the specific tools, blog URL, GitHub repos — this converts "nice-to-have" skills into demonstrated practice
4. In application answers, reference these skills with concrete examples (e.g., "I run my own blog on Hugo with Git-based workflows and Markdown")
5. The Portfolio section is the critical bridge that proves capability without requiring prior employment in the target role

## Support → Infrastructure / Cloud Support Engineer

Lateral move within support, but toward roles heavy on Linux, Kubernetes, networking, virtualization, and cloud infrastructure (e.g. Mirantis, Chainguard, PlanetScale, Imply). The candidate's experience is *already* support engineering — the reframe shifts vocabulary from "SaaS ticket resolver" to "infrastructure escalation engineer."

| Support Framing | Infrastructure Support Framing | Why It Works |
|---|---|---|
| "Resolve complex technical cases" | "Diagnose complex production issues across cloud infrastructure, server environments, and API integrations" | "Production issues" and "infrastructure" are infrastructure vocabulary |
| "Primary escalation resource" | "Highest-tier escalation engineer, functioning as the bridge between Support and Engineering for critical customer-impacting defects" | Mirantis/Chainguard JDs use "escalation engineer" language |
| "Troubleshoot and resolve customer issues" | "Reproduce customer issues in controlled lab environments, confirm bug reports with detailed technical evidence, and deliver fully scoped defect documentation to Engineering with root cause analysis" | Lab reproduction is a core infrastructure support task — JDs explicitly ask for it |
| "Write scripts for automation" | "Write and maintain Bash/Python scripts for automated diagnostics, issue reproduction, and environment configuration across diverse Linux server environments" | Frames scripting as infrastructure tooling, not just task automation |
| "Use Mode/Grafana for data analysis" | "Leverage Grafana for system performance profiling (CPU, memory, disk I/O) and trend identification across customer environments" | System profiling is a JD requirement — reframe analytics as performance monitoring |
| "Document solutions in knowledge base" | "Develop runbooks, internal documentation, and escalation workflows in Confluence to scale resolution of complex infrastructure issues" | "Runbooks" is infrastructure vocabulary |
| "Collaborate with Engineering" | "Coordinate with development teams to discuss customer issues, suggest improvements, and drive resolution of product-level defects" | Matches infrastructure JD language exactly |
| "Support SaaS customers" | "Support enterprise customers with critical infrastructure deployed across cloud and on-premises environments" | Infrastructure roles care about deployment context |

**Critical resume moves for this pivot:**
- Summary must lead with "Linux systems administration, cloud environments, and complex escalation engineering" — not "technical support engineer with 10+ years"
- Skills section: Linux & Systems FIRST, Networking SECOND, Scripting & Automation THIRD. Drop frontend/API from prominence.
- Add networking as a dedicated skills category (TCP/IP, DNS, firewall, VPN) — infrastructure JDs universally require "expert understanding of networking"
- Add system performance profiling (CPU, memory, disk I/O) to Observability skills
- Emphasize "lab environments" and "reproduction" across all roles — infrastructure support is diagnosis-heavy
- De-emphasize ticket volume and SLA metrics — infrastructure roles care about technical depth, not throughput
- Keep Python prominent — many infra companies (Mirantis, Chainguard) require reading Python logs

## Support → CX Leadership / Support Management

Reframes escalation ownership and mentoring as *team leadership*. The key move: you haven't had the title, but you've done parts of the job.

| Support Framing | CX Leadership Framing | Why It Works |
|---|---|---|
| "Primary escalation resource for complex issues" | "Served as primary escalation contact governing resolution of high-impact customer issues" | "Governing" implies operational ownership |
| "Managed 10-12+ cases daily" | "Handled high-volume workload while maintaining quality benchmarks and SLA compliance" | Removes "tickets," adds operational language |
| "Mentored and trained engineers" | "Coached and developed team capability; trained new hires on tools, processes, and customer communication" | Coaching = management vocabulary |
| "Wrote playbooks and documentation" | "Built internal documentation, playbooks, and scalable support processes to reduce repeat escalations" | Process-building framing |
| "Partnered with Engineering and Product" | "Served as customer insights advocate — surfacing patterns to inform product roadmap decisions" | Strategic partnership framing |
| "Live customer calls for real-time troubleshooting" | "Led high-visibility customer escalations with empathy and precision, communicating across technical and executive audiences" | Matches JD language directly |
| "Used Mode/Grafana to identify patterns" | "Defined and monitored support metrics (time to first response, resolution, deflection, root-cause closure)" | Data-driven CX leadership |
| "Investigated API and integration issues" | "Managed technical operations across the stack — APIs, authentication, cross-platform environments" | Broader operational framing |

**Critical resume moves for this pivot:**
- Summary must lead with leadership language: "Customer Experience Leader" or "CX professional" — not "Senior Support Engineer"
- Skills section: Leadership & Team Development FIRST, Cross-Functional Collaboration SECOND, Technical platforms THIRD
- Experience bullets: emphasize *team development*, *process optimization*, *KPI ownership*, *cross-functional strategic partnership*
- Flag the title gap honestly in cover letter — "I haven't had the Head of title yet, but I've been doing parts of this job for years"

## General Reframing Rules

1. **Lead with the outcome, not the task.** "Resolved tickets" → "Maintained 95% CSAT across enterprise accounts"
2. **Use the target role's vocabulary.** Read 3-5 JDs for the target title and extract recurring terms.
3. **Add the missing cross-functional partner.** Support says "Engineering"; SE says "Engineering, Product, and GTM."
4. **Scale the framing.** Support says "daily cases"; SE says "concurrent customer engagements."
5. **Never fabricate.** Reframe what you *did* — don't invent what you *didn't do*.

## Application Question Strategies

### "Most Difficult Client Win"
Structure: Technical depth → cross-functional collaboration → business outcome
- Pick an escalation that threatened a renewal/expansion
- Show the technical investigation (API, integration, scripting)
- Show the cross-functional coordination (Engineering, Sales, Product)
- End with the business result (renewed, expanded, prevented churn)
- Leave a TODO for specifics only the candidate knows (customer name, deal size, industry)

### "Re-Invent Something at Your Job"
Structure: Problem observation → ambitious solution → tie to target company
- Pick a process that's broken or suboptimal
- Show you've already started fixing it (championed AI tools, built automation)
- Acknowledge what you'd do differently with hindsight
- Connect it to the target company's domain (e.g., "This is directly relevant to what Metronome is building — usage-based billing generates complex support scenarios that would benefit from proactive knowledge systems")
